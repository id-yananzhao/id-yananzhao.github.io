## Research Team Decision

Quick recap of the debate  

Bull’s strongest points  
• 27 % YoY revenue growth to almost $30 B, with data-center/AI still in early ramp.  
• New Ryzen-AI and MI300 accelerator lines give AMD a real seat at the most explosive part of semis—AI inference and training.  
• Free-cash-flow positive ($1.5 B) and no balance-sheet stress, so R&D and cap-ex can be funded internally.  
• Technical trend still constructive (50-day MA well above 200-day), suggesting secular rather than speculative strength.  
• Management execution under Lisa Su has a multi-year track record of hitting road-maps and stealing share from Intel.

Bear’s strongest points  
• Semiconductor demand is cyclical; AI “gold-rush” could cool and the PC market is already saturated.  
• Forward P/E near 55 and net margin <10 % leave little room for an execution misstep; any earnings miss would compress the multiple quickly.  
• NVIDIA dominates AI GPUs; AMD is still a challenger, not the incumbent.  
• Short-term momentum has rolled over (10-day EMA below price, MACD/RSI weak), raising the probability of a pull-back into the next earnings print.  
• Social-media enthusiasm can reverse violently when expectations prove excessive.

Decision—Buy  

Why I side with the bull  
1. Secular tailwind trumps cyclicality.  AI/data-center demand is growing far faster than the normal semiconductor cycle contracts.  AMD’s MI300 orders from hyperscalers are public evidence that the company is becoming a genuine second source to NVIDIA, not an also-ran.  
2. Execution track record.  Over the last seven years AMD has met or beaten guidance in 22 of 28 quarters while taking server CPU share from Intel.  That consistency lowers the probability of an outright earnings “miss,” the bear’s biggest fear.  
3. Valuation in context.  A 55× P/E on a company growing the top line 25-30 % and expanding gross margin is high but not egregious; NVIDIA trades >70×, Broadcom >25× with single-digit growth.  If AMD gets anywhere close to consensus 2026 EPS, the multiple falls into the 30s without the stock price having to rise.  
4. Balance-sheet strength.  With $1.5 B FCF and net cash, AMD can fund its AI gamble without threatening shareholders with dilution or excessive leverage—something Intel cannot currently claim.  

A Hold would be justified only if near-term technicals outweighed the structural story; I don’t believe they do.  A Sell is flat-out incompatible with the company’s growth runway and improving fundamentals.

Investment plan  

1. Position sizing  
   • Allocate a 4 % portfolio weight (moderately aggressive growth position).  
   • Split the entry: 2 % immediately, 1 % after the upcoming earnings call if guidance is reaffirmed/raised, 1 % on any 8-10 % pull-back (e.g., toward the 100-day MA).  

2. Risk control  
   • Initial stop-loss 18 % below blended cost to protect against an earnings shock.  
   • Re-evaluate if P/E expands above 65 without a commensurate bump in forward EPS—don’t let valuation detach from reality.  
   • Hedge tactically by writing covered calls one to two months out when implied volatility spikes (a lesson from past errors where I ignored easy premium capture).  

3. Monitoring checklist  
   • MI300 revenue run-rate vs. NVIDIA H100/H200; look for AMD to reach a double-digit share of AI GPU TAM by late 2025.  
   • Gross-margin progression; target >54 % by 2H-25.  Any reversal here is a red flag.  
   • Data-center CPU share (IDC numbers each quarter); continued gains vs. Intel confirm the “two-engine” growth story.  
   • Macro watch: if enterprise cap-ex budgets roll over, be ready to trim the position by one-third; don’t repeat the mistake of staying fully invested through an obvious down-cycle.  

4. Exit strategy  
   • Base-case price target 18–24 months out: $240 (25× projected 2026 EPS of ~$9.50).  
   • Trim half the position if it reaches target ahead of schedule, let the remainder run with a trailing stop 15 % off the highs.  

By combining a phased entry, clear risk parameters, and ongoing fundamental checks, we capture the upside of AMD’s AI/data-center optionality while protecting the portfolio from the valuation-compression risk highlighted by the bear.
